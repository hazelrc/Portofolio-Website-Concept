Skills Spotlight

Unique skills I’ve learned

I’ve been exploring a mix of coding and design to see how they connect on the web. On the structural side, I learned to build pages with clean HTML and style them with CSS, adding components like grids, cards, forms, and navigation bars. I’ve also practiced building different layouts to make sure pages stay responsive and easy to read on both phone and desktop. Along the way, I worked on small projects such as a personal portfolio, a blog template, and a product showcase. These projects were not large, but they gave me experience in planning features, fixing errors, and keeping the design consistent across pages.

I also experimented with typography, color schemes, and accessibility to see how design choices change the way people interact with a site. Even small adjustments, like line spacing or contrast, made a noticeable difference to the overall experience. I started learning animations and transitions too, adding hover effects, flip cards, and smooth scrolling that made the pages feel more dynamic without needing JavaScript. On the visual side, I created simple icons, banners, and logos using basic image editing and vector graphics tools, which gave my sites a more personal look. Each project added something new to my skill set, from working with flexbox layouts to applying semantic tags, and these lessons continue to help me design cleaner and more engaging websites.

Unique skills I’m pursuing

In the future, I want to deepen my main area of study, which is web design and development, so I can apply it directly in professional settings. My goal is to move beyond simple layouts and start building more complex, functional websites with features like forms, galleries, and responsive grids that adapt to all screen sizes. I plan to strengthen my understanding of clean code practices, accessibility, and maintainable CSS structures, so my work can scale up and remain consistent.

Alongside technical coding, I also want to improve my design sense. Understanding typography hierarchy, color balance, and user interface flow. These visual skills will support my main focus, ensuring that every site I create is not only functional but also appealing and user-friendly. For example, I’d like to design websites that communicate brand identity clearly or provide smooth experiences for visitors.

My approach is to treat web design as a practical skill set, not just a hobby. That means applying it to real scenarios, such as creating a client’s portfolio, building a small business site, or designing a personal blog. Over time, I want my abilities to grow into a balanced mix of technical coding and creative design, ready for professional and practical use in the web industry.

Fun Facts

I’ve always learned best by experimenting, and one of my habits is keeping short “learning logs” where I write in plain language what I figured out each week. It started as a way to remember CSS tricks and layout fixes, but it also motivates me by showing steady progress. In class, I used to stay quiet, but I now challenge myself to ask at least one question per tutorial, even if it feels simple, and this has helped me catch mistakes early and build confidence. Outside of study, I enjoy travelling, especially to nature spots like parks, coastal paths, and mountain trails. I also spend time working on small Arduino projects, wiring sensors or displays and coding sketches to create little interactive gadgets. Debugging these sketches feels similar to fixing a broken layout, requiring patience and problem-solving. Another hobby of mine is 3D design, where I model nature-inspired objects and experiment with light, shadow, and camera angles to improve presentation. Even though these interests are different, they all connect back to design: travelling trains me to notice details, Arduino teaches me to think logically, and 3D design sharpens my sense of structure and composition. Together, they remind me that consistent, hands-on practice leads to meaningful progress in both web design and life.
